Rose opens this one with a mumbled false start, like he caught himself mid-thought, and that small gesture sets the tone for everything that follows: what comes next is as unguarded as anything he has put down. As an opener, it is a deliberate act of disarmament. There is no fanfare, no establishing shot, just a man standing in front of a kitchen mirror noticing that 'all the years I kept ahead are suddenly at my side.' The lyric does something genuinely difficult, finding the exact texture of aging's quiet arrival, not the dramatic kind but the everyday kind, and the track earns its build honestly, starting spare and conversational before the guitars open up into something warm and slightly worn at the edges. The guitar solo that closes it out feels less like a show of force than a long exhale. It runs with more forward momentum than the reflective mood might suggest, and that tension between the driving rhythm and the still, clear-eyed lyric is where the song lives. What it accomplishes as an opener is harder than it looks: it tells you exactly what kind of album you are in, and it makes you want to stay.
