Mad River earns its running time by doing what the best grief-soaked rock does: it builds. The verses move at a brisk clip in D major, clean guitars and a restrained vocal carrying lyrics that are genuinely good, lines like 'she said you'll break, but still be free' landing with the plainspoken weight of early John Prine. Then the chorus opens up into full-force distortion and the whole thing lifts the way a late-period Tom Petty track lifts, not through tricks but through commitment. At nearly six minutes it threatens to overstay, but the final repeat of 'as all things do, eventually' pulls it back to something earned rather than indulgent. A highlight.
