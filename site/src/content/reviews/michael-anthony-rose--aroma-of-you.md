Rose finds the whole architecture of long-term love in the smallest possible details: a lip print on a coffee cup, a sweater in the back seat, someone singing off-key in bed. The song works because it earns its chorus rather than announcing it. Verses settle into something close and conversational, then the arrangement opens wide right around the one-minute mark, background harmonies stacking up behind a soulful lead that carries the hook with real authority. In F minor at a brisk pace, it has no business feeling this warm, and yet it does, landing somewhere between a Babyface slow-burn and classic Lionel Richie without being beholden to either.
