Sitting at the album's midpoint, 'That's the Question' arrives just as the record has opened up with the wider view of 'They Don't Know,' and it uses that momentum to go somewhere quieter and more inward. The question Rose is asking here, who am I when I'm not needed, is one that most people sidestep their whole lives, and he plants it dead center and refuses to let you look away. The lyric 'Maybe work became my language / And the tools became my will' is the kind of plain-spoken precision you get from John Hiatt or early Guy Clark when they are working at their best. The production serves it well: spare in the verses, just enough lift in the choruses that the emotional weight lands without tipping into sentimentality. After the outward gaze of its predecessor, this one pulls the focus back to the first person singular, and that alternating rhythm of self and world is starting to feel like a deliberate structural strategy. It also sets up what comes next: once you have asked who you are when you are not needed, the album has no choice but to answer.
