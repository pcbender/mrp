Rose uses the figure of Clark, a fixer from an older America, to map something harder to name: the slow erosion of collective identity into private certainty that still leaves a box empty. The lyric 'Once we, then me, now hollow' does the heavy lifting with real economy, and the chorus hook, built around that contradiction in the title, earns its repetition. At a steady 80 BPM in G major, the warm fingerpicked guitar keeps things intimate without turning precious, and the dynamic lift into the chorus lands the way early Don Henley could, melody opening up just as the stakes rise. A highlight.
