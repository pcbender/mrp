Track ten is where the album's second half starts to consolidate, and 'What To Do With Me' is exactly the right vehicle for that work. The production does exactly what the lyrics ask: the verses are nearly bare, just breath and piano, so when the chorus breaks open it feels less like a musical decision and more like the inside of someone's skull going loud all at once. STAB leans into the tension between the frantic pace and the subject matter, a song about the terror of stillness running at a clip that never quite lets you settle, which is the point. The lyric that lands hardest is 'I've been afraid of being small / afraid of being ordinary, afraid of being left,' and she earns it, because by the time it arrives the arrangement has made the emotional logic feel inevitable rather than confessional. It closes on a whispered 'stay,' no resolution, no bow tied, and that restraint is the best call on the track. In sequence it deepens what 'My Hands Don't Know' opened: the second half of this record is willing to sit with discomfort rather than resolve it too quickly.
