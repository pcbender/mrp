Rose has written self-examination songs before, but 'It's in Me Too' is the one where the ledger gets read out loud without flinching, and it arrives at exactly the right moment. The album has spent two tracks on what it feels like to be seen poorly by the world; now comes the harder turn, examining what the person at the center of all that indignation might owe in the other direction. The line 'I've used my pain like proof that I see deeper than the rest' is the kind of honesty that can collapse a song if the performance does not match it, but Rose earns it. The production delivers: a close-miked confession that breaks open into full-band declaration right when the lyric demands it, architecture closer to Springsteen at his most direct than anything indie or country. The restraint in the verses is real, not strategic, which is what makes the release genuine. As a penultimate passage into the album's final stretch, it clears the air of everything self-protective, which is exactly what the record needs before it can close.
