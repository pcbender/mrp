The cruel joke of 'Slow Down' is that it runs at a breathless clip while pleading for a breather, and Rose clearly knows it. In the wake of the opener's kitchen-mirror intimacy, this one widens the lens without losing the personal register: where 'I'm Old Now' is a man noticing himself, 'Slow Down' is that same man noticing the world he has to move through. The crunch owes a debt to late-period Petty, and the lyric is unusually precise: 'Every room is built for younger knees' lands harder than any generic lament, and 'Sleep is thin, and stairs have opinions' is the kind of line that makes you put down your phone. The guitar solo earns its place rather than just filling a section, and the final chorus shift, where 'every screen is asking me to learn again' becomes 'every face is fluent in a world that doesn't check if I can read,' is the kind of small revision that separates a good songwriter from a careless one. Back-to-back with the opener it could feel like too much of the same key, but the shift from interior to exterior saves it: two songs, same tempo, two different kinds of reckoning.
