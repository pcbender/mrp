 ### 1. High-Quality Lyrics Text ( .txt )

• Why it helps: Transcription models (like Whisper) are excellent but prone to minor errors, especially on sung
vocals where words are stretched or blended (e.g., hearing "Hana" as "hand out").
• Critic Benefit: Having the official lyric sheet allows a critic to dissect the exact poetic structure, rhyming
scheme, and metaphor choice without guessing.

### 2. Contextual Metadata (Artist, Title, & Release Context)

• Why it helps: A critic never reviews a song in a vacuum.
• Critic Benefit: Knowing the Artist Name, Song/Album Title, and Release Year allows the review to place the song
in the artist’s discography (e.g., "a departure from their previous acoustic style").

### 3. Genre, Subgenre, & Sonic References

• Why it helps: Knowing the intended genre helps evaluate the song against its peers.
• Critic Benefit: It allows the critic to discuss specific genre tropes and name-drop influences (e.g., "draws
heavy production cues from Porter Robinson and Tycho" or "reminiscent of 80s dream-pop").

### 4. Background Story & Artistic Intent

• Why it helps: Music critics look for the "why" behind the song.
• Critic Benefit: Knowing what inspired the track (e.g., "written during a transitional period during a road trip
down the Road to Hana") or the production details (e.g., "recorded live in a bedroom studio using analog
synthesizers") provides a narrative hook for the review.

### 5. High-Fidelity Audio Format

• Why it helps: Compressing audio to 128 kbps MP3 introduces a low-pass filter at 16 kHz, removing the "air" and
high-end sparkle of the mix.
• Critic Benefit: A lossless format like WAV or FLAC (or high-bitrate 320 kbps MP3) makes it much easier to analyze
the production quality, panning, spatial depth (reverb/delay), and low-end mixing of the bass and kick.
